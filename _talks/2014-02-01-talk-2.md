Advancing substance use epidemiology requires analytical systems capable of both accurate prediction and rigorous evaluation of intervention effectiveness. This presentation introduces a methodological framework that combines predictive analytics, Bayesian updating, and causal inference to support decision-making in acute substance use and addiction care.

Using routinely collected administrative and clinical data, the framework integrates gradient-boosted trees, regularized logistic regression, and adaptive Bayesian learning to estimate short-term risks associated with overdose, relapse, and crisis-related healthcare utilization. The model is intentionally calibrated toward high sensitivity, reflecting the substantial clinical and public health costs associated with missed high-risk cases.

Beyond prediction, the framework embeds causal inference strategies—including regression discontinuity and difference-in-differences designs—to evaluate the effectiveness of intervention thresholds and care pathways in real-world settings. This integration allows the system to move beyond static prediction toward continuous learning and evidence-generating clinical decision support.

Simulation analyses demonstrate strong predictive performance across varying prevalence scenarios and misclassification cost structures. The work highlights how epidemiology, biostatistics, and artificial intelligence can be combined to produce transparent, adaptive, and operationally relevant systems for addiction and mental health care.
