Clinical decision-making in acute substance use and addiction care requires balancing two competing risks: failing to intervene when severe harm is imminent and over-intervening in ways that strain resources or disrupt patient-centered care. Current approaches often rely on static tools or unstructured clinical judgment, limiting transparency and consistency in high-pressure settings such as emergency departments and addiction treatment services.

This talk presents the Adaptive High-Sensitivity Triage Model (AHSTM), a data-driven framework designed to support rapid, high-stakes decision-making in addiction medicine. The framework integrates ensemble machine learning, Bayesian updating, and causal inference methods to estimate short-term risk for overdose, relapse, and crisis escalation while prioritizing sensitivity to minimize missed high-risk cases.

The model incorporates three core components: (1) safety gates for immediate high-risk scenarios, (2) high-sensitivity dynamic risk estimation, and (3) threshold-based intervention translation for escalation pathways such as OAT initiation, inpatient stabilization, and intensive follow-up care. Simulation analyses demonstrate that the framework achieves sensitivity exceeding 95% while reducing unnecessary high-intensity interventions relative to traditional rule-based approaches.

The presentation also discusses how regression discontinuity and difference-in-differences methodologies can be embedded into operational systems to continuously evaluate intervention effectiveness and improve real-world addiction care outcomes.
